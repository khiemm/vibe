Talking about **health** (/helth/) in English is useful because people often need simple words fast. You may want to describe a **cough** (/kof/), a **fever** (/ˈfiː.vər/), a **stomachache** (/ˈstʌm.ək.eɪk/), a **headache** (/ˈhed.eɪk/), or a feeling of **stress** (/stres/). In daily life, we usually do not need medical language first. We need clear words for **symptoms** (/ˈsɪmp.təmz/), basic **medicine** (/ˈmed.ɪ.sɪn/), and short explanations that other people can understand.

## Common health vocabulary

- **physical health** (/ˈfɪz.ɪ.kəl helth/): the condition of your body
- **mental health** (/ˈmen.təl helth/): your emotional and psychological well-being
- **illness** (/ˈɪl.nəs/): a sickness or unhealthy condition
- **disease** (/dɪˈziːz/): a more formal word for a medical condition
- **symptom** (/ˈsɪmp.təm/): a sign that shows you are sick
- **medicine** (/ˈmed.ɪ.sɪn/): something you take to treat an illness
- **painkiller** (/ˈpeɪnˌkɪl.ər/): medicine that reduces pain
- **tablet** (/ˈtæb.lət/): a small solid form of medicine
- **prescription** (/prɪˈskrɪp.ʃən/): a doctor's written order for medicine
- **recovery** (/rɪˈkʌv.ər.i/): the process of getting better

## Everyday illnesses and symptoms

These are common words people use in conversation:

- **cough** (/kof/): "I have a bad **cough** and my throat hurts."
- **fever** (/ˈfiː.vər/): "She has a **fever**, so she is staying in bed."
- **sore throat** (/sor throʊt/): "If your **sore throat** gets worse, you should rest more."
- **runny nose** (/ˌrʌn.i ˈnoʊz/): "He has a **runny nose** and keeps sneezing."
- **stomachache** (/ˈstʌm.ək.eɪk/): "I got a **stomachache** after dinner."
- **headache** (/ˈhed.eɪk/): "I cannot focus because I have a **headache**."
- **back pain** (/bæk peɪn/): "She works at a desk, so she sometimes gets **back pain**."
- **dizziness** (/ˈdɪz.i.nəs/): "If you feel **dizziness**, sit down immediately."
- **fatigue** (/fəˈtiːɡ/): "Constant **fatigue** can be a warning sign."
- **stress** (/stres/): "Too much **stress** can affect both body and mind."
- **anxiety** (/æŋˈzaɪ.ə.ti/): "He feels **anxiety** before important meetings."

## Talking about medicine

When people feel sick, they often talk about simple treatment first:

- **take medicine** (/teɪk ˈmed.ɪ.sɪn/): "Did you take your **medicine**?"
- **take a painkiller** (/teɪk ə ˈpeɪnˌkɪl.ər/): "I took a **painkiller** for my headache."
- **see a doctor** (/siː ə ˈdɑk.tər/): "You should **see a doctor** if the pain continues."
- **get a prescription** (/ɡet ə prɪˈskrɪp.ʃən/): "The doctor gave her a **prescription**."
- **rest** (/rest/): "Sometimes you just need to **rest**."
- **drink plenty of water** (/drɪŋk ˈplen.ti əv ˈwɔː.t̬ɚ/): "If you have a fever, **drink plenty of water**."

## Physical health and mental health

People often separate **physical health** and **mental health**, but they affect each other. If you do not sleep well, your body may feel weak. If you are under heavy **stress**, you may get a **headache**, **fatigue**, or even a **stomachache**. Good **mental health** does not mean you feel happy all the time. It means you can manage pressure, recover from difficult days, and ask for help when you need it.

Useful sentences:

- "My **physical health** is okay, but my **mental health** has not been great lately."
- "If you ignore **stress** for too long, it can affect your sleep."
- "She took a break because her **mental health** was getting worse."

## A light focus on conditional sentences

Health advice often uses **conditional sentences**, especially real-life situations.

### Zero conditional

Use this for general truths and common results.

- "If you **do not sleep** enough, you **feel** tired."
- "If people **have** a fever, they often **drink** more water."

### First conditional

Use this for a real future possibility.

- "If your cough **gets** worse, you **should see** a doctor."
- "If I **feel** dizzy again, I **will call** the clinic."
- "If she **takes** the medicine on time, she **will recover** faster."

This structure is common in health advice because it sounds practical and natural.

## Short reading

Last week, I felt tired all day and had a mild **fever**. At first, I thought it was just **fatigue**, but then I developed a **sore throat** and a dry **cough**. I took some **medicine**, drank warm water, and stayed home. I also noticed that my **stress** level was high because I had not been sleeping well. If I do not rest enough, I usually get worse. If my symptoms continue tomorrow, I will **see a doctor**.

## Useful word pairs

- **catch a cold** (/kætʃ ə koʊld/)
- **have a fever** (/hæv ə ˈfiː.vər/)
- **develop symptoms** (/dɪˈvel.əp ˈsɪmp.təmz/)
- **take medicine** (/teɪk ˈmed.ɪ.sɪn/)
- **feel better** (/fiːl ˈbet̬.ɚ/)
- **make a recovery** (/meɪk ə rɪˈkʌv.ər.i/)
- **mental exhaustion** (/ˈmen.təl ɪɡˈzɔːs.tʃən/)
- **physical pain** (/ˈfɪz.ɪ.kəl peɪn/)

## Final note

When you learn health English, focus on words people actually say. Start with **symptoms**, basic **medicine**, and short conditional sentences for advice. If you can say what hurts, how long it has lasted, and what happens if it gets worse, your English becomes much more useful in real life.
